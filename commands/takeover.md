# /takeover

Take over an existing project with a clear understanding of the real state.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Gather

Ask the user:
- What documents do you have from the previous PM? (charter, schedule, status reports, risk register, budget, contracts, meeting minutes — anything)
- Why is the project being handed over? (PM left, project in trouble, organizational restructure, normal rotation)
- What have you been told about the project's current state?
- Who are the key people you will be working with?

### Step 2: Document Cross-Check → `/consistency-check` or `/document-audit`

Analyze the inherited documents. Feed them through the consistency check to find:
- What the documents agree on (the baseline facts)
- Where documents contradict each other (the real problems)
- What is missing entirely (the hidden risks)

### Step 3: Contradiction Report → `/contradiction-report`

Produce the formal contradiction register. This is the new PM's ammunition — it shows where the previous reporting did not match reality and where decisions need to be made.

### Step 4: Gap Analysis → `/gap-analysis`

Identify what information is missing. The gaps tell you what conversations you need to have in your first two weeks.

### Step 5: Stakeholder Map → `/stakeholder-map`

Map the political landscape. Pay special attention to:
- Who wanted the previous PM gone (if applicable)
- Who has been making decisions in the absence of a PM
- Who has been sidelined and may be hostile
- Who will test the new PM in the first week

### Step 6: Charter Review or New Charter → `/charter-review` or `/charter`

If a charter exists, review it against current reality. It may be outdated, sanitized, or fiction. If no charter exists, produce one — the new PM needs a decision document to anchor their authority.

### Step 7: Takeover Brief

Synthesize everything into a takeover brief:

**Project Reality vs. Reported State** — What the documents say versus what the cross-check reveals. Where the two diverge, the reality is usually worse.

**Critical Unknowns** — The top 5 things you do not know that you need to find out in the first two weeks. For each: what to ask, who to ask, and why it matters.

**Stakeholder Landscape** — The political map. Who is an ally, who is a threat, who is undecided. What the new PM's first conversation with each should accomplish.

**Immediate Risks** — The risks that need attention in the first 30 days. Not the full risk register — just the ones that could blow up before you have time to settle in.

**First 30 Days Plan** — Prioritized actions for the first month. Focus on: establishing authority, filling information gaps, building key relationships, and addressing the top 2-3 risks.

## Output

A takeover brief that gives the new PM:
1. An honest picture of what they are inheriting
2. A map of who they need to talk to and what to discuss
3. A prioritized action plan for the first 30 days
